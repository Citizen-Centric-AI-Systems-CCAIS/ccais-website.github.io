Keeping buildings comfortable usually costs energy — heating in winter, cooling in summer, and ventilation to keep the air fresh and healthy. But how do we know which designs and retrofits actually deliver a comfortable, healthy indoor environment without driving up carbon emissions and bills? It is a surprisingly hard question to answer, because comfort and air quality depend on a building's materials and design, its heating, cooling and ventilation systems, the weather outside, and the everyday habits of the people living and working inside.

This project uses artificial intelligence to model the building physics behind low-carbon comfort. By monitoring real buildings over long periods and training data-driven models on the resulting measurements, it aims to predict how thermal comfort and indoor air quality respond to different building designs, retrofits and comfort strategies — and to test where AI models can complement, or improve on, traditional building-physics simulations. The goal is to give residents, designers and policymakers better tools for creating buildings that are genuinely comfortable and healthy to live in, while supporting the transition to net zero.
